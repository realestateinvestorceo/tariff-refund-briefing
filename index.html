NEW DETAIL — MARCH 31 Phase 1 exclusions now clearly defined

Plain English: CBP spelled out which import shipments WON'T be handled in the first wave of refunds — and why.

The March 31 report spells out exactly what the CAPE system will NOT handle in its first phase. Here's each exclusion and why it's excluded:

Excluded from Phase 1:

• Import transactions flagged for reconciliation — These are shipments where the importer told CBP "I'll finalize the details later." They can't be refunded until those details are settled.

• Shipments tied to a drawback claim — "Drawback" means the importer already applied for a different type of refund (for re-exporting goods). CBP needs to untangle which refund applies first.

• Shipments with an open formal dispute filed with CBP — If an importer already filed a formal challenge (called a "protest") about their tariff charges, CBP won't process the refund until that dispute is resolved. Ironically, importers who proactively challenged their tariffs are now locked OUT of Phase 1.

• Shipments not in CBP's online system (ACE) — Some older import transactions were filed on paper or in a legacy system. CBP's new refund tool can't see them.

• Shipments with anti-dumping duties still pending — These involve extra tariffs on goods sold below fair market value. A separate government agency (Commerce Department) hasn't finished its review yet, so CBP can't close the books.

Good news — scope expanded: Import transactions that were previously on hold (suspended, extended, or "under review" status) are now expected to be ACCEPTED in Phase 1. This is a change from earlier guidance and means more importers qualify for first-wave refunds.

Fully closed import transactions: Shipments where CBP already formally closed the books AND the 180-day window to challenge them has expired — these are explicitly NOT in Phase 1. CBP says they "may" be addressed later but gave no timeline.

What this means for us: The exclusion list creates the Phase 2 opportunity — complex shipments that need real expertise to recover. But the protest exclusion is a new wrinkle: importers who proactively disputed their tariffs are now stuck waiting longer than those who didn't.

KEY DETAIL — MARCH 31 CBP confirms 45-day processing window after submission

Plain English: Once you submit your refund claim, expect to wait up to 45 days to get your money — and CBP can send the refund to someone other than the original importer if authorized.

CBP indicated it may take up to 45 days to review and formally close each import transaction after a CAPE claim is submitted — as long as there are no compliance red flags. This sets the expectation for the claim-to-cash timeline once the system goes live.

Refunds will be issued to the company legally listed as the importer on customs paperwork OR a designated third party via a CBP authorization form (Form 4811). This confirms the third-party payee model works — relevant for any claim-purchase or advance service.

MARCH 27 12,300+ refunds rejected because importers haven't set up bank deposits

Plain English: The government is trying to send refund money to thousands of importers, but it can't — because those importers haven't registered their bank account with CBP.

CBP's official bulletin (#68179006) confirmed that over 12,300 approved refunds have been rejected because importers haven't set up electronic bank deposits with CBP. This is up from 7,700 on March 6. The number is growing weekly as CBP closes more import transactions while importers remain unenrolled.

What this means for us: This is the bank registration service opportunity in action — real money sitting in a holding pattern because importers haven't completed a signup process. Every week that passes, more refunds stack up in "rejected" status.

MARCH 27 Trade court expands refund order to include fully closed import transactions

Plain English: The court said even importers who missed the window to challenge their tariff charges can still get refunds — but the government might appeal this, and CBP isn't including these in the first wave regardless.

Judge Eaton amended the trade court order to authorize CBP to reopen and correct IEEPA tariff charges even for import transactions that were already formally closed — including ones where the 180-day window to file a formal dispute had already expired. This is expansive and good for importers, but the government has until May 4 to appeal. CBP's March 31 filing confirmed they are NOT including these transactions in Phase 1 regardless.

What tariffs are covered?

Eligible for refund: All IEEPA-based tariffs — fentanyl/trafficking tariffs on China (up to 145%), Canada & Mexico (25%), "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs (10–50% on 57+ countries), tariffs on Brazil, India, Venezuela, and Russia.

NOT eligible: Steel/aluminum/auto tariffs (Section 232), China tech tariffs (Section 301), safeguard tariffs (Section 201), and anti-dumping duties (extra charges on goods sold below fair market value). These remain in force and are NOT part of this refund.

WEBINARS & EDUCATION What the webinar circuit is telling importers

Multiple firms have run webinars — Holland & Knight, Citrin Cooperman/Blue Tiger International, Procopio, and several trade associations. The consistent messaging from all of them:

1. "Act now, don't wait." Every firm leads with urgency — deadlines to file formal disputes are ticking, bank registration with CBP is mandatory, and the CAPE refund system could launch any day.

2. "This is not automatic." They all emphasize that importers must take action. CLA specifically says "refunds won't be automatic — importers must take steps in CBP's online system (ACE)." This is the core message that creates demand for recovery services.

3. "Work with your customs broker." Notably, several firms (CLA, Benesch) are pointing importers back to their existing brokers first, which suggests the advisory firms see themselves as the tax/accounting/legal layer on top, not the primary filing channel.

4. "Timing is the greatest unknown." Benesch explicitly listed "knowns vs. unknowns" — and timing dominated the unknowns column. This uncertainty is what creates the opening for claim-purchase and advance services.

5. "Watch out for downstream liability." Several firms are now warning that consumers may sue importers who received refunds but had already passed the tariff costs on to customers through higher prices. This is a new angle that wasn't part of the initial conversation.

Nobody is doing exactly what JZ would do — combining high-velocity outbound sales with trade refund processing. Flexport is tech-heavy but doesn't do outbound sales to small importers. TRG has the right model but lacks JZ's scale and track record. Baker Tilly is polished but priced for the upper mid-market. The gap is exactly where we identified it: the lower mid-market importer ($10K–$500K refund) who needs someone to call them, explain the opportunity, walk them through bank registration with CBP, and handle the CAPE filing. Nobody is owning that segment with real sales firepower yet.

Five key risks to understand

These are the structural reasons this opportunity has real limits.

1

The window is months, not years

The tariff refund is a one-time event. Once CBP's CAPE system processes the backlog (estimated 6–12 months), the business evaporates. You must acquire customers, process claims, and collect fees in a compressed timeline.

2

CBP is automating you away — on purpose

CBP is building the CAPE system specifically to minimize the need for outside help. It's designed for importers to file their own claims. Thompson Coburn's own advisory acknowledges CAPE may reduce recovery costs. Each system update erodes the value of hiring an intermediary.

3

Eligibility is binary — no gray areas

IEEPA refund eligibility is a simple data match against CBP's own records: did you pay IEEPA tariffs or not? There's no room for "we'll find money you didn't know you had" — the government already knows exactly who paid what.

4

Many prospects can't actually file

Many U.S. companies that economically paid tariffs aren't the company legally listed as the importer on customs paperwork — a foreign manufacturer or freight forwarder is. When a foreign seller handles customs instead of the U.S. buyer (known as "DDP" shipping), the U.S. company has zero legal standing to claim refunds from CBP. Your sales team will waste time on companies that can't file. Must verify who the listed importer is before engaging.

5

Consumer class actions create downstream liability

At least four class action lawsuits target companies that passed tariff costs to consumers through higher prices and now seek refunds — alleging "double recovery." Importers receiving refunds may face their own legal exposure. Client agreements need protective provisions.

Sign up for ImportGenius, PIERS, or Descartes Datamyne. These are databases that track every shipment entering the U.S. — who received it, what country it came from, what type of product, and estimated volumes. This is how you find importers who paid IEEPA tariffs.

Budget ~$5,000–$15,000/year depending on platform and data depth.
Build the prospect list

Filter the trade data: shipments from China, Canada, Mexico, India, Brazil between Feb 2025–Feb 2026. Isolate shipments with IEEPA-specific tariff codes (the 9903.01.XX and 9903.02.XX code ranges). Rank by volume. Remove Fortune 500 companies (they have counsel). Target companies with 10–500 import shipments.

Goal: 5,000–10,000 qualified leads with verified import activity and estimated refund amounts.
Cross-reference bank registration status

Use CBP's public reporting to identify which importers have NOT set up electronic bank deposits with CBP — these are the warmest leads because they literally cannot receive money without help.

3
Build the service stack
Days 3–10
Service 1: Bank registration assistance (the door-opener)

Create a streamlined process for helping importers set up their account in CBP's online system (called ACE) and register their bank account for electronic refund deposits. This is administrative paperwork — no customs license needed. Charge a flat fee ($500–$1,500) or use it as a free/low-cost lead-gen for the full service.

Pitch: "You're owed $X. The government literally cannot pay you because you're not registered. We fix that in 48 hours."
Service 2: Refund claim preparation and filing

Build the workflow to take your client's import records (reports exported from CBP's system), identify which charges are IEEPA tariffs (separating them from other tariffs like steel/aluminum that aren't being refunded), clean up the data, and generate the spreadsheet file that CAPE requires for submission. Many importers paid multiple types of tariffs on the same shipment — untangling which charges are refundable is where the real value is.

Charge 3–10% of the recovered amount (you only get paid if they get paid), or flat fee per import transaction ($5–$25 each).
Service 3: Formal dispute filing for at-risk import transactions

For import transactions approaching the 180-day deadline to challenge charges, file a formal dispute with CBP (called a "protest") through partner counsel. Think of this like filing an appeal before the deadline expires. This is where the law firm partnership matters — you identify the at-risk transactions, they file the legal paperwork.

Learn CBP's online system inside and out

Someone on the team must physically walk through the entire process: creating an account in CBP's ACE system, registering for electronic bank deposits, exporting import records, and (once live) submitting a CAPE refund claim. Document every step, screenshot every screen. This becomes your training material and your process documentation.

4
Launch the sales engine
Days 7–14
Build a simple landing page

One-page site: "Are you owed an IEEPA tariff refund?" Lead capture form. Refund estimator tool (input: number of import shipments, average shipment value, origin country → estimated refund). Trust signals from partner law firm.

Outbound sales campaign

Use the prospect list from Phase 2. Email + phone outreach to CFOs and supply chain/logistics managers. The pitch is simple: "The Supreme Court says you're owed $X. 94% of importers aren't set up to receive their refund. We handle everything."

Hire 3–5 sales development reps (SDRs) who are comfortable with the "government owes you money" pitch.
Channel partnerships with customs brokers

Customs brokers are the companies that handle import paperwork for businesses. Many of the 11,500 licensed brokers are overwhelmed or don't want to handle the refund process for their smaller clients. Approach regional brokers with a referral fee arrangement — they send you their clients who need help, you handle bank registration and CAPE filing, they get a cut.

This is potentially the highest-leverage channel — brokers have trust and data but may lack bandwidth.
Verify the prospect is actually the listed importer

Before any engagement, verify the prospect is the company legally listed as the importer on customs paperwork — not a company whose foreign supplier handled customs for them. Check Box 27 (Importer number) and Box 30 on the CBP import form (Form 7501). If the foreign seller handled customs, the U.S. company has no legal standing to claim — reject immediately regardless of how much they paid economically.

5
Scale and evolve
Days 14–30 and beyond
CAPE launch day operations (target: mid-April)

Be ready to submit spreadsheet files for your first batch of clients on day one. Monitor for errors closely — the first wave will surface formatting issues that affect everyone. Fast correction and resubmission = competitive advantage.

Develop the claim-purchase arm (optional, capital-intensive)

For importers who want cash now rather than waiting 45 days, offer to buy their refund claims at 80–90 cents on the dollar. They get immediate cash, you collect the full refund later. Requires working capital but creates immediate revenue. Texas Capital Bank and Tariff Recovery Group are already doing this.

Pivot to recurring trade compliance advisory

As the one-time refund window closes, transition clients into ongoing services: identifying other refund opportunities, tariff classification reviews, compliance audits. The tariff refund is the customer acquisition event — the recurring revenue is the real business.

This is the strategic play that makes the whole venture worthwhile even if refund fees alone are modest.
Monitor for Phase 2 CAPE opportunities

Complex import transactions excluded from Phase 1 — those involving anti-dumping duties, warehouse shipments, and transactions tied to other refund programs — will need more hands-on assistance. These are higher-value, higher-complexity claims where intermediary expertise genuinely matters.
