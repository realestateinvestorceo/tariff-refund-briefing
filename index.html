NEW DETAIL — MARCH 31 Phase 1 exclusions now clearly defined

The March 31 declaration spells out exactly what CAPE Phase 1 will NOT handle:

Excluded from Phase 1: Entries flagged for reconciliation (Type 09), entries designated on a drawback claim, entries with an open protest, entries not filed in ACE or without liquidation status in ACE, and AD/CVD entries with pending Commerce liquidation instructions.

Change from earlier guidance: Entries with suspended, extended, or "under review" liquidation status are now expected to be ACCEPTED in Phase 1 — this was previously listed as excluded. This expands the Phase 1 scope.

Finally liquidated entries: Explicitly NOT in Phase 1. CBP says they "may" be addressed in a later phase but gave no timeline.

What this means for us: The exclusion list creates the Phase 2 opportunity — complex entries that need real expertise. But it also means some importers who filed protests are ironically locked OUT of Phase 1 until their protest is resolved. That's a new wrinkle to watch.

KEY DETAIL — MARCH 31 CBP confirms 45-day processing window after submission

CBP indicated it may take up to 45 days to review and liquidate entries after a CAPE declaration is submitted — absent additional compliance concerns. This sets the expectation for the claim-to-cash timeline once the system goes live.

Refunds will be issued to the importer of record OR a designated third party via CBP Form 4811. This confirms the third-party payee model works — relevant for any claim-purchase or factoring service.

MARCH 27 12,300+ refunds rejected for missing ACH enrollment

CBP's official CSMS bulletin (#68179006) confirmed that over 12,300 certified refunds have been rejected because importers haven't provided banking information. This is up from 7,700 on March 6. The number is growing weekly as more entries liquidate while importers remain unenrolled.

What this means for us: This is the ACH enrollment service opportunity in action — real money sitting in a holding pattern because importers haven't completed a registration process. Every week that passes, more refunds stack up in "reject" status.

MARCH 27 CIT expands refund order to include finally liquidated entries

Judge Eaton amended the CIT order to authorize CBP to reliquidate and refund IEEPA tariffs even for entries beyond final liquidation — entries where the 180-day protest window already expired. This is expansive and good for importers, but the government has until May 4 to appeal. CBP's March 31 filing confirmed they are NOT including these entries in Phase 1 regardless.

What tariffs are covered?

Eligible for refund: All IEEPA-based tariffs — fentanyl/trafficking tariffs on China (up to 145%), Canada & Mexico (25%), "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs (10–50% on 57+ countries), tariffs on Brazil, India, Venezuela, and Russia.

NOT eligible: Section 232 (steel/aluminum/autos), Section 301 (China tech), Section 201 safeguards, antidumping/countervailing duties. These remain in force.

What they're actually saying and doing

TECH PLATFORM Flexport — the most aggressive mover

What they built: A free "Tariff Refund Calculator" (tariffs.flexport.com/refunds) that lets any importer upload their 2025 Entry Report and get an instant estimate of their IEEPA refund. They also launched an AI-powered "Audit Your Customs Broker" tool (tariffs.flexport.com/audit) that reviews past filings, finds mistakes, and identifies refund opportunities. Both tools are free and available to non-Flexport customers.

Their pitch: "See exactly what you're owed in 5 minutes." They claim to provide the fastest, most accurate way to calculate refunds. Their CEO said Flexport is "uniquely positioned" as one of the largest customs brokerages in the U.S. to help businesses of all sizes.

Their real strategy: The free calculator is a lead-generation machine. Importers upload their actual entry data to use it — giving Flexport a complete picture of their import activity, tariff exposure, and current broker. It's a Trojan horse to win brokerage business, not just refund fees. They also offer duty drawback services as an upsell.

Threat level for us: High for tech-savvy importers. Low for the mid-market importers who don't even know what ACE is — Flexport's tools assume a level of customs literacy that our target market often lacks.

ERC-STYLE Tariff Recovery Group — the closest ERC analog

Who they are: Founded by Aharon Margolin, a small-business financing veteran — not a customs expert. This is the most directly comparable company to what JZ would build.

Their model (three tiers): (1) Free consultation and audit of your import profile. (2) Managed recovery — they handle the full CAPE filing process. (3) Claim purchase — they buy your refund claim upfront for immediate cash, then handle recovery themselves.

Their pitch: "Don't wait for the government to process your refund. We purchase your anticipated IEEPA duty refund claim upfront — putting cash in your hands now while we handle the recovery." Their FAQ addresses every major objection: legal certainty, timeline uncertainty, finally liquidated entries.

What's notable: They're explicitly positioning the unknown timeline as a selling point for the claim-purchase model: "CAPE is still being built, and a system processing 53 million entries will face technical and validation delays well beyond launch." Smart — they're using uncertainty as an urgency lever.

Threat level for us: Direct competitor in our target segment. First-mover advantage. But their team appears small and they're a new company — not an established brand. JZ's ERC track record ($7.7B processed) would be a stronger trust signal.

LAW FIRM Quinn Emanuel — built a dedicated "Tariff Refund Task Force"

What they built: A full branded landing page (quinnemanuel.com/tariff-refund-task-force) with podcast episodes, webinar recordings, client alerts, and a clear intake flow. They have Dennis Hranitzky publicly saying businesses should "act now" on tariff recovery.

Their pitch: "Companies are confronting legal questions they have never faced before." They emphasize the complexity of deciding whether to sue, wait, or use the administrative process — positioning legal counsel as essential for any strategy.

Threat level for us: Low — they're targeting large enterprises who can afford elite litigation rates. But their messaging frameworks are worth studying for how to create urgency.

BIG FOUR / ADVISORY Baker Tilly — the most polished advisory play

What they built: A dedicated "Tariff Refund & Recovery Services" practice with a proprietary dashboard that consolidates ACE data, broker entries, and payment records. They published a detailed "Readiness Checklist" to drive inbound leads and are running webinars on CFO-level tariff planning.

Their pitch: "Recovery will not be automatic. Outcomes depend on entry liquidation status, protest timing, and documentation quality. Organizations that act early will be better positioned." They position themselves as the serious, compliance-focused option and explicitly warn about the need for audit-ready documentation.

Their angle: They tie IEEPA refunds into broader trade advisory — transfer pricing, inventory accounting, SALT implications, M&A considerations. This is the "tariff refund as customer acquisition for ongoing advisory" model we identified in our action plan.

Threat level for us: Medium — they're targeting upper mid-market ($100K+ claims) and have brand credibility we'd lack at launch. But their pricing likely excludes the smaller importers we'd target.

WEBINARS & EDUCATION What the webinar circuit is telling importers

Multiple firms have run webinars — Holland & Knight, Citrin Cooperman/Blue Tiger International, Procopio, and several trade associations. The consistent messaging from all of them:

1. "Act now, don't wait." Every firm leads with urgency — protest deadlines are ticking, ACH enrollment is mandatory, and CAPE could launch any day. This is the same playbook ERC firms used.

2. "This is not automatic." They all emphasize that importers must take affirmative steps. CLA specifically says "refunds won't be automatic — importers must take affirmative steps in CBP's ACE system." This is the core message that creates demand for intermediaries.

3. "Work with your customs broker." Notably, several firms (CLA, Benesch) are pointing importers back to their existing brokers first, which suggests the advisory firms see themselves as the tax/accounting/legal layer on top, not the primary filing channel.

4. "Timing is the greatest unknown." Benesch explicitly listed "knowns vs. unknowns" — and timing dominated the unknowns column. This uncertainty is what creates the opening for claim-purchase and advance services.

5. "Watch out for downstream liability." Several firms are now warning that consumers may sue importers who received refunds but passed tariff costs through. This is a new angle that wasn't part of the initial conversation.

What this tells us about positioning

Nobody is doing exactly what JZ would do — combining ERC-level sales velocity with trade refund processing. Flexport is tech-heavy but doesn't do outbound sales to small importers. TRG has the right model but lacks JZ's scale and track record. Baker Tilly is polished but priced for the upper mid-market. The law firms are focused on litigation, not mass administrative processing.

The gap is exactly where we identified it: the lower mid-market importer ($10K–$500K refund) who needs someone to call them, explain the opportunity, walk them through ACH enrollment, and handle the CAPE filing. Nobody is owning that segment with real sales firepower yet.

Five risks that make this different from ERC

These are the structural reasons this opportunity cannot scale like ERC did.

1

The window is months, not years

ERC ran for 3+ years with rolling eligibility quarters and retroactive filing. The tariff refund is a one-time event. Once CAPE processes the backlog (6–12 months), the business evaporates. You must acquire customers, process claims, and collect fees in a compressed timeline.

2

CBP is automating you away — on purpose

The IRS never built a modern ERC system, leaving intermediaries essential. CBP is building CAPE specifically to minimize the need for outside help. Thompson Coburn's own advisory acknowledges CAPE may reduce recovery costs. Each system update erodes the intermediary value proposition.

3

Eligibility is binary — no gray areas to exploit

ERC intermediaries thrived by interpreting ambiguous eligibility rules in clients' favor. IEEPA refund eligibility is a simple data match against CBP's own records. There's no room for "we'll find money you didn't know you had" — the government already knows exactly who owes what.

4

The DDP trap eliminates many prospects

Many U.S. companies that economically paid tariffs aren't the Importer of Record — a foreign manufacturer or freight forwarder is. These companies have zero legal standing to claim refunds from CBP. Your sales team will waste time on companies that can't file. Must verify IOR status (Box 27/30 on CBP Form 7501) before engaging.

5

Consumer class actions create downstream liability

At least four class action lawsuits target companies that passed tariff costs to consumers and now seek refunds — alleging "double recovery." Importers receiving refunds may face their own legal exposure. Engagement agreements need indemnity provisions.

Why ACH enrollment is the fastest path

Over 300,000 importers cannot receive refunds because they haven't set up electronic payment in CBP's ACE system. Over 12,300 certified refunds have already been rejected per CBP's March 27 bulletin, and only ~21,000 importers (~6%) have enrolled. This is an immediate, concrete pain point that doesn't require customs expertise to solve — and it creates a natural upsell into CAPE filing assistance.

The smarter long play

Use tariff refund services as a customer acquisition channel for ongoing trade compliance advisory, duty drawback services, or supply chain consulting. Turn a one-time event into a recurring business. That strategic pivot is more defensible and sustainable than trying to replicate the ERC playbook in a fundamentally different market.

Use the prospect list from Phase 2. Email + phone outreach to CFOs and supply chain/logistics managers. The pitch is simple: "The Supreme Court says you're owed $X. 92% of importers aren't set up to receive their refund. We handle everything."

Hire 3–5 SDRs with ERC sales experience. They already know the "government owes you money" pitch cadence.
Channel partnerships with customs brokers

Many of the 11,500 licensed brokers are overwhelmed or don't want to handle the refund process for their smaller clients. Approach regional brokers with a referral fee arrangement — they send you their clients who need help, you handle ACH setup and CAPE filing, they get a cut.

This is potentially the highest-leverage channel — brokers have trust and data but may lack bandwidth.
IOR verification gate

Before any engagement, verify the prospect is the actual Importer of Record (not buying DDP). Check Box 27 (Importer of Record number) and Box 30 on CBP Form 7501. Reject DDP prospects immediately — they have no legal standing regardless of economic burden.

Days 14–30 and beyond
CAPE launch day operations (target: mid-April)

Be ready to submit CSV files for your first batch of clients on day one. Monitor validation errors closely — the first wave will surface formatting issues that affect everyone. Fast correction and resubmission = competitive advantage.

Develop the claim-purchase arm (optional, capital-intensive)

For importers who want cash now rather than waiting 45 days, offer to purchase refund claims at 80–90 cents on the dollar. Requires working capital but creates immediate revenue. Texas Capital Bank and Tariff Recovery Group are already doing this.

Pivot to recurring trade compliance advisory

As the one-time refund window closes, transition clients into ongoing services: duty drawback identification, tariff classification reviews, FTZ consulting, compliance audits. The tariff refund is the customer acquisition event — the recurring revenue is the real business.

This is the strategic play that makes the whole venture worthwhile even if refund fees alone don't match ERC returns.

Complex entries (AD/CVD, warehouse, drawback) excluded from Phase 1 will need more hands-on assistance. These are higher-value, higher-complexity claims where intermediary expertise genuinely matters.
